New Construction BRRRR Strategy

Ironwood Renovations · Build · Rent · Refinance · Repeat · Every 4 Months

Overview

Ironwood Renovations' new construction strategy is built around one repeating cycle: build a duplex, place quality tenants, refinance at appraised value, pull capital back out, and use it to fund the next build. By targeting a four-month construction timeline and a DSCR cash-out refinance at completion, the company aims to recycle the same capital base across multiple builds — growing a rental portfolio without continuously raising new equity.

Each completed duplex remains in the Ironwood portfolio as a long-term income-producing asset, managed through Ironwood Property Management LLC. The equity built into each property becomes the foundation for future leverage — lines of credit, blanket mortgages, and portfolio loans — as the portfolio scales.

Core Thesis

Build once. Hold forever. Recycle capital. Let compounding do the rest. The goal is not to sell — it is to own as many doors as possible while the refi proceeds fund the next build.

Phase 1 — Land Acquisition and Permits (Month 0–1)

The lot must be identified, under contract, or already owned before breaking ground. Using land that was purchased for cash as the first collateral position gives Ironwood the ability to leverage that equity through the hard money lender — receiving the land's value back as part of the construction draw. Permits must be pulled before Month 1 so construction starts on time. Any permit delays push the entire cycle back and increase carrying costs.

Phase 2 — Construction (Month 1–4)

Jeff Sarabia leads construction with Ty Jackson providing field oversight. The build budget must stay within $280,000–$295,000 all-in to make the refinance math work. Every dollar over budget either reduces the capital recycled at refi or requires additional out-of-pocket capital to close the gap. Hard money draws are released in stages tied to construction milestones — foundation, framing, mechanical rough-in, drywall, finish work, and final inspection. Draw requests must be submitted on time and inspections passed promptly to avoid delays in funding.

Phase 3 — Certificate of Occupancy and Tenant Placement (Month 4–5)

Jordan Sarabia begins marketing both units no later than Month 3 — before construction is complete. The goal is to have signed leases in hand at or before the certificate of occupancy is issued. Lenders want to see a rent roll, not projections. Having tenants in place before applying for the refinance strengthens the appraisal, satisfies DSCR underwriting requirements, and eliminates vacancy risk during the seasoning period. Target tenants: credit score 620 or above, income at least three times monthly rent, verifiable rental history.

Phase 4 — Seasoning (Month 5–6)

Most DSCR lenders require the property to be owned and stabilized for three to six months before approving a cash-out refinance. During this period, the duplex is cash-flowing — rent income covers the hard money interest payments (or in Ground Floor's case, interest is deferred until the refi closes). Ty Jackson initiates contact with the DSCR lender at the start of this period, orders the appraisal, and begins assembling the refinance package: lease agreements, rent collection records, insurance documentation, title commitment, and entity documents.

Phase 5 — Cash-Out Refinance and Capital Recycle (Month 6–7)

At a stabilized ARV of $420,000 and a 75% LTV DSCR loan, the refinance produces $315,000. After paying off the hard money balance of approximately $295,000, Ironwood walks away with roughly $20,000 in recycled capital and a long-term DSCR mortgage at approximately $1,750 per month. With two units renting at $1,400 each, monthly gross rent is $2,800 — producing approximately $1,050 per month in cash flow after the mortgage payment. The recycled $20,000 is deployed immediately into the next land acquisition, starting the cycle again.

Lender Stack

Two lenders are required for each cycle: a hard money lender to fund construction, and a DSCR lender to refinance at stabilization. These are two separate relationships that must both be in place before breaking ground on any project.

Construction Phase — Hard Money Lenders

Ground Floor (Ben Spinek, 470-228-2326) is the preferred construction lender for this strategy. At 9.5% interest with deferred payments until sale or refinance, Ground Floor eliminates the cash drain during construction and seasoning. Their 18-month maximum term provides sufficient runway for the full cycle. The only restriction to plan around is that Ground Floor does not finance sewer tap fees — these must be paid out of pocket or through a separate credit line at closing. Duke Allen & Co (Joseph Castaneda, 305-522-6126) at 11–15% is the backup lender if Ground Floor is unavailable on a given project.

Fund the Flip (Dylan, 216-815-1723) at 10.99–11.49% requires a GC license between Ironwood and the builder. This must be resolved before Fund the Flip can be used — either by Jeff obtaining the appropriate Georgia GC license or through a licensed GC arrangement. This is a prerequisite action item before the first build.

Hold Phase — DSCR Lenders

After the property is stabilized and seasoned, a Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR) loan replaces the hard money. DSCR lenders do not underwrite based on personal income — they lend based on the property's rent coverage ratio. A DSCR of 1.25 or above is standard (meaning rent must be at least 1.25 times the monthly mortgage payment). At $2,800 gross rent and a $1,750 mortgage payment, the DSCR is approximately 1.6 — well above the threshold. Target DSCR lenders: Lima One Capital, Kiavi (formerly LendingHome), Visio Lending, and CoreVest Finance. Begin conversations with at least one DSCR lender before the first build begins.

The Land Problem and How to Solve It

The single biggest bottleneck in a four-month repeating cycle is having the next lot ready before the current build finishes. If Duplex #1 refinances in Month 7 and the next lot is not under contract, the recycled capital sits idle while you search for land — breaking the cycle and costing months of compounding time.

The solution is to stay one lot ahead at all times. Before breaking ground on any duplex, the next lot must already be identified and either under contract, optioned, or owned. The $20,000 recycled from Duplex #1's refi should be committed to Lot #2 before the ink is dry on the refinance closing documents. The following approaches are available depending on capital position:

  • Cash purchase — cleanest option; use refi proceeds to buy the next lot outright before it is gone
  • Seller financing — negotiate a small down payment with the seller carrying the balance; allows you to control the lot without tying up full capital
  • Option agreement — pay a small option fee (typically $1,000–$5,000) to lock in the right to purchase the lot within a set window; preserves capital while securing the site
  • Buy 2–3 lots now — if the current capital position allows, purchasing the next two or three lots immediately eliminates the land sourcing bottleneck for the next 12–18 months

Risk Factors and How to Manage Them

Risk 1 — Build Cost Overrun

If the all-in build cost exceeds $295,000, the refi proceeds may not fully pay off the hard money balance — requiring Ironwood to bring cash to the refi table. Mitigation: Fixed-price subcontractor contracts where possible. Jeff tracks actual vs. budget weekly. Any scope change requires both Members' approval before work proceeds.

Risk 2 — Appraisal Comes in Below ARV

If the DSCR appraisal comes in below $420,000, the refinance proceeds shrink and less capital is recycled. Mitigation: Build to the market, not below it. Research comparable sales (comps) before finalizing the build spec. Have a contingency plan — if the appraisal is low, challenge it with comp data or wait and re-appraise after additional appreciation.

Risk 3 — Vacancy at Refi Time

DSCR lenders require stabilized occupancy. If units are vacant at refinance, the loan may be declined or terms worsened. Mitigation: Jordan begins marketing at Month 3, one month before CO. Pre-lease both units before the certificate of occupancy is issued. Offer a move-in incentive (first week free) if needed to accelerate placement.

Risk 4 — Interest Rate Environment

If DSCR rates rise above 7.5–8%, the monthly mortgage payment increases and cash flow per door shrinks or turns negative. Mitigation: Monitor DSCR rates quarterly. If rates are unfavorable at seasoning, delay the refi by 60–90 days rather than locking in an unfavorable rate. Ground Floor's 18-month term provides this flexibility.

Risk 5 — Land Pipeline Dries Up

If the next lot is not identified before the current refi closes, recycled capital sits idle and the cycle breaks. Mitigation: Ty Jackson maintains an active lot pipeline at all times — minimum two lots identified at any point. Begin lot sourcing for Lot #2 on Day 1 of Duplex #1 construction.

Long-Term Vision

Three years of consistent execution at four-month intervals produces nine to twelve duplexes — eighteen to twenty-four rental doors — without ever raising outside equity. At that scale, the portfolio generates $9,000–$12,000 per month in cash flow and holds $900,000–$1,200,000 in equity. That equity position unlocks a portfolio blanket mortgage or line of credit that can fund multiple simultaneous builds — compressing the cycle from one duplex every four months to two or three per year in parallel.

At the ten-duplex threshold, Ironwood's rental portfolio qualifies for institutional DSCR products with lower rates and higher LTVs, further improving the refinance math. The remodeling business continues generating operating capital, meaning the construction pipeline is never solely dependent on refi proceeds. The two businesses — remodeling and new construction — feed each other: remodeling builds the trade credit, subcontractor relationships, and supplier accounts that reduce construction costs; the rental portfolio provides the balance sheet that attracts better lender terms on future deals.

This is the compounding machine. Build it once. Run it forever.
